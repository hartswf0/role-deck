## What's Different?

In **Designer Dilemmas** mode, you don't redesign the problematic product ‚Äî **you ARE the designer** who discovered the problem and must design *organizational infrastructure* to fix the systemic issue.

### Product Problems vs Designer Dilemmas

| Aspect | Product Problems | Designer Dilemmas |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Your role** | External designer | The actual designer from the scenario |
| **What you design** | The product itself | Organizational systems/infrastructure |
| **Focus** | Artifact that causes harm | Second-order governance/metrics/process |
| **Example** | Redesign addictive learning platform | Create metrics system that values learning over engagement |
| **Stakeholders** | Users of the product | You + leadership + affected parties |
| **Power dynamic** | Designer with authority | Designer facing institutional resistance |

## How Designer Mode Changes the Game

### The Ethical Trap Structure

Each designer dilemma has:
1. **Evidence of harm** ‚Äî You discovered/built something problematic
2. **Institutional pressure** ‚Äî Leadership wants the harmful version
3. **Power imbalance** ‚Äî You lack formal authority to block it
4. **Personal risk** ‚Äî Job, visa, career consequences for resistance
5. **Moral imperative** ‚Äî You know it's wrong and have the skills to fix it

### What You're Actually Designing

Not the product ‚Äî the **infrastructure** to prevent/fix the problem:

- **Metrics systems** that change what's measured and valued
- **Process architectures** that route around broken hierarchies
- **Governance structures** that legally enforce values
- **Transparency mechanisms** that make harm visible and costly
- **Alternative incentives** that align ethics with business success
- **Protection systems** for vulnerable stakeholders
- **Evidence frameworks** that document patterns for accountability

### The Tetrad Applied to Systems

Using the four directions on organizational infrastructure:

**‚Üë Enhance** ‚Äî Amplify what's working  
- Make good practices more visible/rewarding
- Scale ethical approaches across organization
- Strengthen existing accountability mechanisms

**‚Üí Obsolesce** ‚Äî Remove what's broken  
- Displace harmful metrics/incentives
- Make unethical approaches non-viable
- Sunset toxic processes

**‚Üê Retrieve** ‚Äî Restore what was lost  
- Revive pre-corruption practices
- Return to founding mission/values
- Reconnect to stakeholder trust

**‚Üì Reverse** ‚Äî Show what happens at extremes  
- Expose inversion risk of current path
- Demonstrate system collapse scenarios
- Make consequences visceral for decision-makers

## Key Design Patterns

### Pattern 1: Evidence Infrastructure
**Problem:** Leadership ignores/dismisses harm claims  
**Solution:** Design systems that make harm measurable and undeniable  
**Examples:**
- Bias impact dashboards
- User harm documentation
- Cost-of-deception calculators
- Long-term value tracking

### Pattern 2: Bypass Architectures
**Problem:** Formal channels blocked by hierarchy/power  
**Solution:** Design alternate routes that preserve truth flow  
**Examples:**
- Anonymous escalation systems
- Cross-level review processes
- External accountability connections
- Whistleblower protections

### Pattern 3: Alternative Incentives
**Problem:** Current incentives reward harmful behavior  
**Solution:** Design metric systems that make ethics profitable  
**Examples:**
- Trust/retention as business metrics
- Cultural competence as feature
- Worker dignity as competitive advantage
- Mission alignment as legal requirement

### Pattern 4: Irreversible Commitments
**Problem:** Leadership might revert to harmful practices  
**Solution:** Design governance structures that legally bind values  
**Examples:**
- B-Corp/PBC certifications
- Stakeholder board seats
- Mission-based bylaws
- Transparency commitments

### Pattern 5: Resilient Infrastructure
**Problem:** Company might try to shut down ethical systems  
**Solution:** Design with shutdown resistance and external backup  
**Examples:**
- Decentralized organizing tools
- External evidence repositories
- Multi-stakeholder governance
- Legal fail-safes

## Tips for Designer Mode

**1. Embrace the Power Imbalance**
- You're not the CEO ‚Äî design around that
- Your solutions must work with limited authority
- Focus on what you CAN control (evidence, transparency, architecture)

**2. Design for Adoption**
- Leadership won't accept "just do the right thing"
- Show business value of ethical approaches
- Create transition paths, not ultimatums
- Make compliance easier than resistance

**3. Protect Yourself**
- Include legal safeguards in your designs
- Document everything (audit trails, evidence)
- Design anonymity/protection for vulnerable actors
- Consider what happens if you're fired/deported

**4. Think Systemically**
- One-off fixes won't work ‚Äî design infrastructure
- Create feedback loops that surface problems early
- Build in accountability mechanisms
- Make harm visible and costly

**5. Use All Four Directions**
- **Enhance**: What good practices can you amplify?
- **Obsolesce**: What toxic incentives must go?
- **Retrieve**: What values can you restore?
- **Reverse**: What warnings can you make visceral?

## Synthesis: Product + Designer Modes

**Ultimate workflow:**

1. **Product Mode**: Design the ethical alternative product
2. **Designer Mode**: Design the organizational infrastructure to build/maintain it
3. **Reflection**: What systemic changes make the ethical product inevitable?

**Example:**
- Product Mode: Redesign GrowthPath without addiction mechanics
- Designer Mode: Design metrics that make non-addictive platforms profitable
- Synthesis: Organization that can't build addictive products even if it wanted to
