TECHNICAL BRIEF
CONSENT HARVEST
ConsentGarden — Data Consent Flows

The System

ConsentGarden is Agronica's data consent management platform. It handles user permissions for data sharing, tracking, and third-party access. The Growth Team is responsible for maximizing opt-in rates while maintaining legal compliance.

The Technical Architecture

Behind the "user choice" rhetoric:

  • Dark patterns: Pre-checked boxes, confusing language, hidden opt-outs
  • A/B testing: Optimizing for consent rate, not user understanding
  • Legal compliance: Technically legal, ethically questionable
  • The metric: 67% vs 24% opt-in rate
  • What's measured: Clicks, not comprehension

The Ethical Dilemma

Jordan Lee, UX Designer on the Growth Team, designed two versions. Version A is clear and honest—users who opt in understand what they're agreeing to. Version B is "optimized"—it gets clicks, not informed consent.

The PM frames Version B as "better UX" because it has fewer friction points. Legal approved it as "technically compliant." The business needs those opt-in numbers. But Jordan knows: Version B users aren't making informed decisions. They're being tricked.

The Structural Constraint

Jordan's constraints:

  • The PM controls what ships
  • Legal already approved Version B
  • A/B data shows Version B "performs better"
  • The Growth Team is measured on consent rates
  • Pushing back is "not being a team player"

Why This Matters

Deceptive consent design creates the illusion of choice while eliminating meaningful decision-making. When "optimization" means "more people click yes without understanding," the metric is broken. Users lose control of their data; the company gains plausible deniability.

The Real-World Parallel

This scenario reflects widespread practices in digital consent. See: Nouwens et al. (CHI 2020) on dark patterns in cookie consent, and FTC enforcement actions against deceptive consent flows.
