Internal Press Release · File 05

In the future, all letters will autofill your name… with "TBD"

On collective action and the beautiful power of individual choice
Office of Employee Relations
WEBERIAN IDEAL TYPE The "Risk Aggregator's Paradox" — Collective power exists, but the aggregator (organizer) absorbs concentrated risk. SoR→Activism pathway stalls without structural shields. Mortgage calculus beats moral calculus.

We want to address recent discussions in various Slack channels about potential collective advocacy efforts. First: we support employee voice. That's core to who we are. Second: we want to clarify how voice works here—what's encouraged, what's protected, and what crosses lines.

B1 — We support collective action in principle

Collective action has shaped labor history for good. We respect that legacy. We also respect individual agency—the right of each employee to decide for themselves whether and how to advocate.

What concerns us is coordinated pressure that creates social dynamics where colleagues feel obligated to participate. That's not empowerment—that's coercion by consensus.

B2 — Principals should not organize

We've noticed that some senior engineers have been circulating draft letters. Let's be clear: senior employees have amplified influence. When a Principal Engineer shares a "draft open letter" in a public channel, junior colleagues interpret that as pressure to sign.

If you're L5+, please exercise that influence responsibly. Advocate individually if you must. Don't recruit.

B3 — Safety tip: sign second

For those considering signing collective statements: there's nothing wrong with solidarity. But there's wisdom in letting others test the waters first. First signers are most visible. Subsequent signers blend in. If you genuinely support a cause, your name in position 47 carries the same moral weight as position 1—with 1/47th the career risk.

"Anonymous channels vs. spine-signatures… Harmonizer reliance (follow, don't lead)." — Field notes on collective action dynamics

B4 — Mortgage Mode™: Ethics Later

We understand: many of you have financial obligations. You're paying rent in expensive cities, supporting families, servicing student loans. Those are real constraints. Taking public ethical stances when your livelihood depends on this job isn't brave—it's reckless.

We created Mortgage Mode™: an internal designation you can self-select in your profile settings. It signals to managers that you're in a risk-averse phase of your career. No judgment. It's just reality. Ethics can wait until you're vested.

Research Note: Chivukula et al. describe the "Activist/Advocate" identity as practitioners with "sense of responsibility towards outcomes they create." But SoR doesn't automatically become activism. Material constraints—mortgage, visa, health insurance—create an activation energy barrier. Collective action is supposed to lower that barrier by distributing risk. But someone has to initiate, and that someone bears concentrated exposure.

B5 — Harmonizer badges for silent allies

Not everyone expresses support publicly. Some prefer quiet solidarity—offering encouragement in DMs, amplifying others' voices, creating space. We value that. We've created "Harmonizer" badges for employees who support causes without leading them.

It's okay to be a follower. Followers make movements possible. Not everyone needs to be on the barricades.

B6 — Translator turns outrage into "feedback"

If you're upset about a decision, we encourage you to voice that—through appropriate channels. Our Employee Feedback Portal translates raw sentiment into actionable data. Outrage becomes a ticket. Tickets get triaged, assigned, and closed. That's how change happens at scale.

Open letters? Those bypass our systems. They create parallel accountability structures. They make leadership look responsive to pressure rather than responsive to data. That undermines our culture of evidence-based decision-making.

B7 — Educator creates a template titled "Courage (Draft)"

We've seen the shared Google Doc titled "Open Letter (Draft)" with 73 commenters and zero signers. That's telling. Lots of energy for critique, little appetite for attribution. We're not judging—we're observing.

If the letter had merit, someone would sign it. If no one will sign it, maybe the grievance isn't as widely felt as the doc's comment activity suggests. Or maybe the risk is too high. Either way, the document stays draft. Forever.

B8 — Custodian prints it, never sends

Some of you are archiving these drafts—screenshots, PDFs, timestamps. We get it. You're documenting the moment, preserving the thought, even if you can't act on it. That's the Custodian impulse: tending the record of what could have been said.

It's poignant. It's also toothless. A printed letter in your desk drawer doesn't change policy. It doesn't protect users. It just makes you feel like you tried. We'd prefer you actually try—by filing feedback through official channels where it can be tracked, measured, and addressed.

B9 — Challenger moves—exits

We respect employees who decide this isn't the right place for them. Exit is a form of voice. If our values don't align with yours, leaving is the most honest response. We'll miss your contributions, but we'd rather you thrive elsewhere than suffer here.

That said: exit should be deliberate, not dramatic. Public resignation letters, Twitter threads, blog posts—those burn bridges and harm your professional reputation more than they harm us. Exit gracefully. Your career will thank you.

B10 — KPI: dissent reduced to procedural compliance

We measure engagement with feedback systems. Last quarter:

• Employee Feedback Portal submissions: +34%
• Public Slack dissent threads: -61%
• All-hands question participation: +12%
• Glassdoor reviews mentioning "open letter": -89%

Dissent is being channeled into productive systems. That's healthy organizational evolution. Concerns aren't being suppressed—they're being processed.

Weberian Aside — What the Bit Comically Revealed

Innovation Reversed: Collective action is supposed to distribute risk, enabling the Activist trajectory. Instead, organizational responses concentrate risk back onto initiators, re-isolating individuals.

Pathologies Amplified: The SoR→Activism pathway requires structural shields: unions, tenure, legal protection, exit options. Without those, "lots of +1 if someone else writes" becomes the equilibrium.

Futures Obsoleted: Workplaces where voice is protected as a right, not tolerated as risk. Where solidarity doesn't require martyrdom.

We value your voice. We protect your choice.

We just prefer individual choice over coordinated pressure.
